# 08 — Async and Tech Friction

> The early-2000s office runs on email, Outlook, Lotus Notes, intermittently functional voicemail, and PCs that take five minutes to boot. The technology is not a tool, it's a *participant* — a slow, partly-broken third party that takes its own time and produces its own surprises.

---

## What's already in the engine that this builds on

- `WorkloadComponent`, `FlowStateComponent`, `StressComponent`.
- `KnowledgeComponent`, `RumorSystem`.
- The world bible's named anchors (the IT closet, where Greg lives; the conference-room TV nobody can connect to; the supply closet's printers nobody knows how to use).

## 8.1 — EmailSystem + EmailMessageComponent

### The human truth

Email is the office's slow nervous system. Most of an office's official record exists in inboxes. Most of the day's emotional friction is generated by inbox content. The tone of an email is misread routinely; the strategic CC is a weapon; the sent-from-the-wrong-account is a real-life nightmare; the reply-all that should have been a reply has destroyed careers. The cc on a small request is a small status move. The recipient who didn't reply for three days has communicated something even if they meant to communicate nothing.

### What it does

Email is implemented as a discrete async messaging system with first-class drama mechanics:

`EmailMessage` entity:

- `sender`, `recipients[]`, `cc[]`, `bcc[]`, `subject`, `body`, `urgencyMarker`, `tone`, `sentAt`, `readBy[]`, `repliedBy[]`.
- `tone` — `neutral | warm | clipped | urgent | passiveAggressive | accidentallyCold`. Tone is set by the author's mood and personality at composition time and is *legible to the engine* but *ambiguous to recipients*.
- `urgencyMarker` — explicit (high-importance flag) or implicit (subject line ALL CAPS, multiple exclamation points).

On receipt, NPCs *interpret* tone with their own personality bias:

- High-neuroticism NPCs over-read negative tone (a neutral email reads as cold; a warm email reads as neutral).
- High-agreeableness NPCs read tone benignly (a clipped email reads as just busy; a passive-aggressive email reads as concerned).
- Low-trust pairs: any ambiguity reads negative.

### Tone-misread events

A `ToneMisreadEvent` fires when interpreted tone differs significantly from authored tone. The recipient's response now has a tone influenced by their misread, which the *author* will then misread back. Email ping-pong escalation is a real thing the system can produce in three exchanges.

### Specific drama events

- **Reply-all-disaster** — an NPC intends to reply to one person, accidentally hits reply-all. The body of the message contains content that was never meant for the wider list. High-noteworthiness chronicle event. Career-defining for some NPCs.
- **Sent-from-wrong-account** — IT closet specifically; rare but devastating.
- **Strategic CC** — sender CC's a third party deliberately to trigger them or to put pressure on the recipient. The CC'd party reads it correctly as a strategic move; their `suspicion` toward the sender bumps.
- **CC-the-boss** — the most aggressive escalation. The recipient knows what just happened.
- **The unanswered email** — recipient hasn't replied in 24, 48, 72 sim-hours. Sender's `irritation` and `suspicion` accumulate. The eventual reply, when it comes, is colored.

### Components

- `InboxComponent` per NPC.
- `EmailMessage` entities.
- `EmailDraftComponent` for in-progress messages — drafted but not sent. The draft an NPC has been holding for two days is a load-bearing component.

### Personality + archetype gates

- The Climber composes carefully. Each email is calibrated. Their tone is rarely misread because they over-soften.
- The Cynic sends terse emails. The terseness is read as rude by half the office; the Cynic doesn't care.
- The Vent (Donna) writes long emails with multiple exclamation points and emoji-substitutes for the era ( :) and !! ). Her tone is unmistakable.
- The Hermit replies in three words. *"Yep, on it."* They get reputation as efficient or as cold depending on the reader.
- The Old Hand's replies arrive when they arrive. Their queue is opaque. Most coworkers trust they'll eventually answer.
- The Founder's Nephew's replies are inconsistent. Sometimes the next day; sometimes a week later; sometimes never. Other NPCs have learned not to wait.

### Cross-system interactions

- **WorkloadSystem:** unread email counts in the inbox are ambient stress.
- **DialogCalcify:** repeated email phrasings calcify per NPC ("Per my last email...") even faster than spoken phrases because email is reviewed.
- **RumorSystem:** email content forwarded with commentary is one of the highest-fidelity rumor transfer mechanisms.
- **KnowledgeComponent:** specific email content is high-confidence first-hand knowledge.
- **PassiveAggression:** the strategic CC and the per-my-last-email pattern are formal-channel passive-aggression.

### What the player sees

An inbox view is one of the most information-rich player surfaces. Reading a single NPC's inbox is reading their week.

### The extreme end

The Climber drafts a complaint email about the Cynic, intends to send it to their manager. The day they finish drafting it, they accidentally hit reply-all on a *different* email, and in the panic of that mistake they hit Send on the draft to the wrong recipient list — including the Cynic. The Cynic reads it in the next ten minutes. Forwards it to Donna. Donna forwards it to three people. By end of day the entire floor knows. The Climber's `status` takes a permanent hit. The Cynic's relationship matrix improves with everyone except the Climber. The chronicle records this for the rest of the save.

### Open questions

- Should email be authored prose or generated? The dialog bible commits to no-LLM. Email content can be a templated fragment system similar to dialog, with templates per tone × context × register. ~50 templates would cover most of office email.
- Should there be a *visible* inbox UI for the player, or only `EmailComposed` and `EmailRead` events the player can inspect?

## 8.2 — InstantMessagingSystem (the early-2000s IM era)

### The human truth

AOL Instant Messenger or its corporate equivalent. The other channel — faster than email, slower than walking-over. The away message as identity broadcast. The buddy list as social map. The *typing* indicator as social pressure (they're seeing me typing right now). The conversation that runs in parallel with a meeting. The screen-name that's now embarrassing.

### What it does

Similar to email but with different cadence: shorter messages, real-time, status-aware.

- `IMSession` between two NPCs while both are at-desk and both have IM client open.
- `AwayMessage` per NPC: the line they have set right now. Most are utilitarian ("in a meeting, back at 3"). Some are character-defining ("..."). Some change frequently and are part of the NPC's voice.
- `TypingIndicator` — visible to the IM partner. The NPC who's typing for thirty seconds and then sends one word has communicated more than they meant to.
- `BuddyListVisibility` — can see who's online. Reading the buddy list is reading who's at desk vs. who's in a meeting vs. who left for lunch. Some NPCs use *invisible* mode strategically.

### Cross-system interactions

- **Flow:** IM windows open during deep-focus tasks are major flow-disruptors.
- **MeetingSystem:** in-meeting IMing is a parallel back-channel — high-trust pairs trade snark via IM during boring meetings. The chronicle should record these.
- **Affair / Crush:** IM is the medium where romantic-tension scripts itself. The away message that's a song lyric directed at one specific person.

### What the player sees

Buddy list state is a quick-read floor map. Active IM conversations are little drama channels.

### The extreme end

The Affair's two participants conduct a months-long parallel relationship through IM during the workday. Their partners (off-screen) don't know. The system records every message. The day a coworker (cluster 5.3 — the over-shoulder reader) catches a glimpse of one party's screen, the entire arc detonates. The chronicle records the visible aftermath; the IM logs are the secret history.

## 8.3 — VoicemailSystem (the dying medium)

### The human truth

In the early 2000s, voicemail is in transition. Some NPCs check it religiously; some haven't checked it in six months. The voicemail light blinking on the desk phone is a small persistent piece of guilt. The voicemail from a vendor four months ago that nobody returned is real. The voicemail from a family member that was important and is still un-listened-to is a slow-burn shame.

### What it does

`VoicemailInbox` per NPC. Messages have:

- `sender`, `urgency`, `lengthSeconds`, `arrivedAt`.
- `listened: bool`.
- The blinking light is an `EnvironmentalNotification` on the desk phone entity — a small `irritation` drip on the NPC every time they look at it.

### Personality + archetype gates

- The Boomer Old Hand checks voicemail twice daily without fail.
- The Millennial Newbie has never checked it once. The light has been blinking since week 1.
- The Climber checks selectively — only if the caller ID is interesting.

### What the player sees

The desk-phone light blinking on certain NPCs' desks. Eventually, an NPC plays an ancient voicemail and reacts. The chronicle records when it was finally listened to.

### The extreme end

The Newbie ignores voicemail for six months. One day, looking for something else, they hit play on the inbox queue. The first voicemail is from a candidate they were supposed to interview five months ago, who is now hostile. The Newbie's `mask` collapses. They sit at their desk holding the phone. The Old Hand walks past, sees the face, knows what just happened (they've been there). They make small-talk to give the Newbie cover. The Newbie eventually gets it together. The Old Hand never mentions it.

## 8.4 — SlowPCAndTechFrustrationSystem

### The human truth

The PC takes five minutes to boot. The application has crashed. The save dialog is greyed-out and the work is gone. The "blue screen" is real and is unrecoverable. The autosave that didn't save. The keyboard key that's been sticking. These are time-thefts plus rage-events that compound through a workday.

### What it does

Each NPC's workstation has:

- `bootTimeSeconds` — set at simulation start; specific to the machine. Some are 90 seconds; some are 5 minutes. Boot-time variability is its own micro-drama.
- `crashProbability` — per sim-hour, scaled by recent activity intensity.
- `dataLossProbability` — chance that a crash takes work with it.

Tech frustration events fire stochastically:

- **Crash:** active task progress reverts a chunk; `irritation` and `stress` spike.
- **Slow boot:** delays start of work session; `flowState` cannot enter for the duration. Happens every morning.
- **Lost-work crash:** highest-stake — current task progress fully gone. Mask drops, lash-out probability spikes for the next hour.
- **Sticky-key:** typing is degraded; tic-like accumulation of irritation.
- **Printer jam:** Cluster 08.5.

### Cross-system interactions

- **GenerationGapSystem:** tech-frustration impact is multiplied for older-cohort NPCs who have less troubleshooting capacity. The Boomer's PC crashing is an emergency. The Millennial's is a brief shrug-and-restart.
- **Greg / IT closet:** every tech event eventually routes to Greg. Greg's workload is an emergent consequence of every other NPC's tech state.
- **WorkloadSystem:** lost-work events directly cost task progress; deadlines slip; downstream stress amplifies.
- **FavorLedgerComponent:** Greg fixing a coworker's PC is one of the most-frequent favor-generating events in the simulation. Greg is in massive positive favor balance with most of the floor and never calls anything in.

### Personality + archetype gates

- The Climber's PC must be perfect at all times. They're the most-likely to escalate to Greg.
- The Hermit fixes their own things and other people's.
- The Cynic accepts that tech is broken and moves on.
- The Newbie panics; the panic itself often makes the problem worse.

### What the player sees

Periodic tech-rage events. Greg moving across the floor to fix things. The chronicle of who-asked-Greg-for-help-this-week.

### The extreme end

Quarter-end Friday. Three NPCs hit lost-work crashes within the same hour. Greg is overwhelmed. The Climber's deadline is in two hours. They escalate to Greg with high `status`-pressure. Greg, in low-extraversion mode, gets snippy. The Climber takes it personally. A relationship that was professional sours over a single high-stress event. The next month, the Climber starts handling small tech things themselves rather than ask Greg, and the floor reads it as a feud even though Greg has already forgotten.

## 8.5 — PrinterSystem (the printer as office demigod)

### The human truth

The printer is a recurring antagonist with its own moods. It runs out of toner. It jams. It claims to be out of paper when it isn't. It prints fifty extra copies. It makes the long-grinding noise that everyone hears. Standing at the printer waiting for a print is one of the oldest office-bonding microspaces — proximity-induced conversation with whoever else is waiting.

### What it does

Printer entity with:

- `state` — `idle | printing | jammed | outOfToner | outOfPaper | broken`.
- `queueDepth` — number of pending jobs.
- `lastJamUserId` — who was last using it when it jammed (the social cost of having jammed it is real even if it wasn't your fault).

Events:

- **Print job sent.** NPC walks to printer, waits. Queue order matters — the person whose job is third has time to check email on the way back, or get coffee, or have a small conversation with whoever else is waiting.
- **Jam event.** Whoever is currently at the printer must clear it (high-conscientiousness) or walk away (low-conscientiousness, leaves it for the next person). Walking-away is a known low-status move that calcifies into reputation.
- **Toner-out event.** Replacing toner is gross and slightly mechanical; the NPC who does it gains small `competence` recognition.
- **Out-of-paper.** Trivial fix; the NPC who notices and refills earns a tiny favor balance with the floor.

### Printer-Adjacent Conversations

The act of waiting at the printer creates a `printerWaiterGroup` proximity zone. NPCs in this group have low-stakes conversations. These tend to be more honest than desk conversations because the situation is informal and the conversation has a known short duration.

### Cross-system interactions

- **FavorLedgerComponent:** printer-fixers accumulate small positive balance. Printer-jammers-and-leavers accumulate small negative balance.
- **PassiveAggression:** the note on the printer ("PLEASE FIX JAMS YOU MAKE") is a perennial.
- **WorkloadSystem:** printer downtime is task-blocking. The day the printer breaks during a quarter-close-out is a chronicled bad day.

### What the player sees

A predictable, recurring small-drama hub. The printer area is one of the better stages in the building.

### The extreme end

The day the printer dies for two full days. The Climber needs hard-copy materials for a presentation. They drive to a Kinko's at lunch. They return three hours late. Their boss notices. The boss doesn't care about the explanation. The Climber's `status` takes a hit. They blame Greg internally even though Greg has been on the phone with the printer vendor for hours. The chronicle records: *the day Greg saved nobody.*

## 8.6 — RingtoneAndPhoneSystem

### The human truth

Cell phones in the early 2000s are flip phones with downloaded ringtones. The ringtone is identity. The forgot-to-mute incident — the loud ringtone in a quiet meeting — is a recurring office mortification. The desk phone with the distinctive ring (each phone different) is part of the floor's audio signature.

### What it does

Each NPC has a `RingtoneProfile`:

- `selectedRingtone` — character data; the song they downloaded.
- `currentVolume` — what they last set it to.
- `isMuted` — what they intended to do.
- `forgetToMuteProbability` — increases when stressed or sleep-deprived.

Phone-rings-in-meeting event: small embarrassment, mask draw, the ringtone *itself* is suddenly identity-revealing. The Climber's ringtone going off in a board meeting is the same shape as the Cynic's ringtone going off — but the Cynic doesn't care and the Climber does.

### What the player sees

Once or twice per save, a phone goes off in a meeting and the office has a small moment.

### The extreme end

The Hermit's phone rings in a meeting. The ringtone is "I Will Survive." The Hermit's `mask` collapses to single-digit. The room laughs but kindly. The Hermit's `belonging` actually *increases* by the event — the floor saw a side of him they didn't expect, and they liked it. The chronicle records *the day Greg's ringtone gave him friends.*

## 8.7 — DialUpAndConnectivityArtifacts (era-specific texture)

The early-2000s setting has specific texture:

- The dial-up handshake noise from someone working from home over a VPN.
- The fax machine that nobody knows how to use anymore.
- The modem light pattern that means something is broken.
- The web pages that take forty seconds to load.
- The download time bar.

These are atmospheric rather than mechanical. They feed the general `WorkstationDelay` state and add character to specific scenes (the day the internet went out for three hours; the fax that never arrived from a vendor and now we're missing the contract).

### What the player sees

A floor that *feels* of the era. Tech-friction events that wouldn't happen in a contemporary office (the fax wait, the dial-up tone) and that ground the simulation in 2002.
